Sam Langford Biography

Sam Langford

Sam Langford was born on March 4,1886 in Weymouth Falls, Nova Scotia, Canada. He passed away on January 12,1956 in Cambridge, Massachusetts, United States. Langford was former boxer who fought professionally in lightweight, welterweight, middleweight and light-heavyweight and finishing as heavyweight. He was named the “Boxer of Half-Century” by the Canadian Press. He was 5’6” but he was known as “the giant killer. In 1917, Langford completely lost the sight of one eye during a loss against Fred Fulton. Remarkably, he would continue fighting with one eye for another nine years, the last few with limited sight out of his one “good” eye. In 1923 he captured the Mexican heavyweight title in a contest at which he had to rely on his handlers to help guide him into the ring and to his corner. Langford’s assistants were so concerned about his eyesight that they wanted to call the fight off, but Langford refused: He needed the money. Sam fought for another two years while his eyesight continued to fail, until in August 1925, in his last professional bout, he was forced to quit in the opening round of a fight when it became obvious that he couldn’t see his opponent at all.

After Boxing

By 1944, Langford was blind. Langford didn't have In 1952,Langford moved back to Boston and quietly lived out the remaining years of his life in a private nursing home. In 1955 Sam became the first boxer to be voted into the Boxing Hall of Fame without having ever won a world championship title, although he held the heavyweight championship title in countries such as England, Australia and Mexico. He passed away on January 12, 1956, just two months before his seventieth birthday and only ten weeks after being enshrined in the Boxing Hall of Fame. At the time of his induction, Langford was the only non–world titleholder to be so honored. Sam Langford never regretted his chosen profession and expressed no bitterness or remorse over the loss of his eyesight. He maintained a keen sense of humor and kind disposition throughout his life and always said that boxing provided him with a wealth of memories. In a statement attributed to him a few months before his death, he said, “Don’t nobody need to feel sorry for old Sam. I had plenty of good times. I been all over the world. I fought maybe 600 fights, and every one was a pleasure!”
